\begin{abstract}
Two-sample testing asks whether two independent samples come from the same distribution. In biostatistics, such differences often involve mixture composition, covariance, morphology, sparsity, or tail behavior rather than a simple mean shift. The paper reviewed in this project studies Mahalanobis aggregated maximum mean discrepancy (MMMD), which combines several kernel MMD statistics through an estimated covariance matrix instead of relying on a single bandwidth. This report reviews the motivation and related literature for kernel two-sample tests, reserves separate sections for the original method, data review, and other group experiments, and presents our representation-level extension on biomedical images. In the representation component, we compare raw pixels, CLIP, DINOv2, and task-trained CNN features on BloodMNIST, PathMNIST, and NCT-CRC. The main empirical finding is that MMMD power is shaped strongly by the representation space: raw pixels are usually sample inefficient, DINOv2 is stronger than CLIP on pathology-style images, a task-trained CNN is most robust on BloodMNIST, and NEW-MMMD improves calibration and covariance conditioning more consistently than it improves power.
\end{abstract}

\section{Background, motivation, and related work}

The two-sample problem tests whether two samples are drawn from the same distribution. Let \(P\) and \(Q\) be two probability distributions on a sample space \(\mathcal{X}\), and suppose we observe independent samples
\[
\mathcal{X}_m=\{X_1,\ldots,X_m\}\sim P,\qquad
\mathcal{Y}_n=\{Y_1,\ldots,Y_n\}\sim Q.
\]
The hypotheses are
\[
H_0:P=Q,\qquad H_1:P\ne Q.
\]
This problem is common in biomedical studies because group differences can be multivariate and structured. In gene expression, microbiome profiles, single-cell measurements, and biomedical images, the difference between two populations may appear as a change in mixture composition, covariance, morphology, or a small subpopulation. A useful test should therefore be nonparametric, valid under the null, and sensitive to alternatives that are hard to specify in advance.

Classical one-dimensional tests such as Wilcoxon, Wald-Wolfowitz, and Kolmogorov-Smirnov are interpretable, but they are mainly designed for ordered observations. Graph-based and distance-based methods extend two-sample testing to multivariate data through constructions such as minimum-spanning-tree tests and energy-distance statistics \cite{friedman1979,sejdinovic2013}. These methods can work well when the distance captures the scientific difference, but distance geometry can become unreliable in high-dimensional or noisy settings, especially when nuisance variation dominates the signal.

Kernel two-sample tests based on maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) give a flexible way to compare distributions. MMD compares the mean embeddings of \(P\) and \(Q\) in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space. With characteristic kernels, population MMD is zero exactly when \(P=Q\) \cite{gretton2012}. The practical difficulty is bandwidth choice. The median heuristic is convenient, but finite-sample power can change sharply with the kernel and its scale. This issue is especially visible in biomedical image tasks because the same biological difference may look small or large depending on whether the observations are represented as raw pixels, CNN features, or pretrained foundation-model embeddings.

Recent work improves kernel two-sample testing in two main ways. One line learns or selects kernels to increase power, but selection can disturb Type-I error control if the same data are used for tuning and testing; data splitting avoids that problem but spends samples on selection \cite{liu2020}. Another line aggregates multiple kernels, such as MMDAgg, to reduce dependence on a single bandwidth \cite{schrab2023}. Chatterjee and Bhattacharya's MMMD follows the aggregation idea but uses the covariance structure among multiple MMD estimates \cite{chatterjee2025}. Instead of asking which bandwidth is best, MMMD asks how to combine several correlated kernel discrepancies into one calibrated statistic.

Our experiments add a representation-level question. When the observations are images, how much of the two-sample power comes from the test statistic, and how much comes from the representation? We keep the testing layer fixed when possible and vary the representation across raw pixels, CLIP, DINOv2, and task-trained CNN features. This separation matters because poor image geometry can make a good kernel test look weak, while a good embedding can make a biologically meaningful difference easier for MMMD to detect.

\section{Numerical extensions to the MMMD framework}

\subsection{Motivation and experimental setup}

The original paper evaluates MMMD under Gaussian mean-shift and variance-shift alternatives. We extend the numerical study to distributional differences common in biostatistics: heavy-tailed contamination, small systematic covariance changes, non-Euclidean sample geometry, and mixture alternatives where only a fraction of the population differs.

All experiments use synthetic data with $d=30$, AR(1) covariance ($\rho=0.5$), sample size $n=100$, multiplier bootstrap with $B=500$, and $N=150$--$200$ Monte Carlo trials. Three kernel families are compared: GEXP (Gaussian kernel with exponential bandwidth grid), LAP (Laplace kernel), and MIXED (combined Gaussian and Laplace). Detailed parameter grids are given in Appendix~\ref{app:numext}.

\subsection{Sensitivity to contamination and variance change}

We've conducted two experiments to test out the sensitivity of MMMD test. The first one is \textbf{contamination}. Let $P=\mathcal{N}(0,\Sigma)$ and $Q=(1-\varepsilon)\mathcal{N}(0,\Sigma)+\varepsilon\cdot t_{10}(0,\Sigma)$. Using GEXP with $r=5$, the minimum contamination fraction detectable at power 0.80 is $\varepsilon_{\min}=0.625$. Power rises steeply in $\varepsilon\in[0.45,0.65]$ from 0.57 to 0.80, while below 0.45 the heavy-tailed component is too dilute to separate (see Figure~\ref{fig:eps-sensitivity} in the appendix).

The second is \textbf{variance scale.} Let $P=\mathcal{N}(0,\Sigma_0)$ and $Q=\mathcal{N}(0,k\cdot\Sigma_0)$ with $k>1$. All three MMMD variants detect $k_{\min}=1.25$ at power $\geq 0.80$; single-kernel Gaussian MMD requires $k_{\min}=1.40$. At a 20\% covariance increase ($k=1.20$), MMMD power is 0.71--0.79 versus 0.18 for the single-kernel baseline --- roughly a four-fold gap (see Figure~\ref{fig:var-sensitivity} and Table~\ref{tab:var-sensitivity}).

\subsection{Type-I error calibration and ROC analysis}

We evaluate Type-I error at bandwidth counts $r\in\{3,5,10\}$. At $r=3$ and $r=5$, all families are calibrated near the nominal 0.05 level. At $r=10$, GEXP inflates to 0.120, LAP remains at 0.050, and MIXED rises moderately to 0.070. The practical guideline is to limit GEXP to $r\leq 5$; the LAP family tolerates larger kernel banks without inflating the null.

ROC analysis at a fixed variance-shift alternative ($k=1.25$) shows all families maintaining false-positive rates near 0.05 with true-positive rates of 0.435--0.500. Power differences across families are small once the alternative is sufficiently strong (see Figure~\ref{fig:roc} and Table~\ref{tab:type1}).

\subsection{Graph-MMMD: construction, calibration, and skewness robustness}

We extend the MMMD framework by building a $k$-nearest-neighbor graph on the pooled sample $\mathcal{X}_m\cup\mathcal{Y}_n$, forming the graph Laplacian $L$, and using the heat kernel $K_t=\exp(-tL)$ as the kernel family. This replaces the Euclidean bandwidth grid with a diffusion-time grid.

Under four null distributions --- Gaussian AR(1) ($\rho=0.5$), scaled Gaussian ($k=1.5$), skew-normal with shape $\alpha=5$, and multivariate Laplace --- empirical Type-I errors are 0.027, 0.020, 0.020, and 0.033, all well below the nominal 0.05 level (see Figure~\ref{fig:graph-type1}). A skewness sweep across standardized skewness 0.00--0.90 (skew-normal, shape parameter $\alpha$ solved via \texttt{uniroot()}) yields Type-I errors of 0.020--0.053 with no monotonic drift, confirming that graph-based kernel aggregation does not over-react to distributional asymmetry under the null (see Figure~\ref{fig:skew-type1}).

\subsection{Reproduction: mixture distribution power}

We replicate the original paper's normal-$t$ mixture experiment (Fig.~2). Let $P=(1-p)\mathcal{N}(0,\Sigma_0)+p\cdot t_{10}(0,\Sigma_0)$ and $Q=(1-p)\mathcal{N}(0,\Sigma_1)+p\cdot t_{10}(0,\Sigma_1)$ with $\Sigma_1=1.25\cdot\Sigma_0$. At $p=0$ (pure Gaussian), Mixed MMMD achieves power 0.96 versus 0.46 for single-kernel MMD. At $p=0.4$, the gap is 0.80 versus 0.31. At $p=1.0$ (pure $t_{10}$), it is 0.58 versus 0.35. MMMD methods provide 2--5 times the power of the single-kernel baseline across all mixture proportions $p$ (see Figure~\ref{fig:mixture} and Table~\ref{tab:mixture}).

\subsection{Summary}

Multi-kernel MMMD aggregation consistently outperforms single-kernel MMD across contamination, variance scaling, mixture, and graph-based alternatives. Three practical guidelines emerge. First, limit GEXP to $r\leq 5$ or use LAP for larger kernel banks to avoid Type-I inflation. Second, the largest relative gain of MMMD over single-kernel MMD appears when the distributional difference is small, making MMMD particularly suitable for subtle biomedical group comparisons. Third, graph-based kernels extend MMMD to non-Euclidean sample geometry without sacrificing calibration, even under strong asymmetry.

\section{CNN representation-assisted MMMD for image two-sample testing}
\label{sec:cnn-mmmd}

Kernel two-sample tests depend not only on the choice of kernel, but also on the representation to which the kernel is applied. We therefore evaluated MMMD on learned CNN features in addition to raw pixels. A small CNN was trained once on an independent labelled split and then frozen. All subsequent tests used cached embeddings; the network was not updated using either two-sample test group.

Two representations were considered. The first was the 128-dimensional penultimate embedding (FC-128), immediately before the linear classifier. The second combined features from a shallow layer, an intermediate layer, and FC-128. For this multilayer representation, we used either one Gaussian kernel per layer, producing three MMD components, or five bandwidths per layer, producing 15 components. MMMD then aggregated these layer--kernel components through the same Mahalanobis statistic used in the original method.

MNIST additive-noise experiments served as an initial implementation check and showed that learned representations could improve power in selected noise settings without a corresponding increase in matched-null rejection. We next applied the same pipeline to PathMNIST. In the class-mixture experiment, \texttt{mix635} and \texttt{mix835} shared labels 3 and 5 but differed by replacing label 6 with label 8. Formal PathMNIST analyses used 10 outer repetitions, 500 independent tests per repetition, and 500 multiplier-bootstrap draws, giving 5000 tests per method and sample-size cell.

For the class-mixture alternative, FC-128 and multilayer G15 were substantially more powerful than raw pixels at \(n=90\) and \(n=120\), while all matched-null rejection rates were below 0.05. The single-Gaussian multilayer representation did not uniformly improve on FC-128, indicating that adding representation levels is not automatically beneficial; performance depends on how the alternative is expressed in the learned feature space.

We then considered a within-class source-vs-external comparison for labels 6 and 8. Here the tissue label was fixed, so the alternative represented center-associated variation rather than a change in class composition. At \(n=50\), multilayer representations were especially sensitive for label 6, whereas raw pixels and FC-128 were already highly sensitive for label 8. Some small-sample internal-null checks for multilayer G15 were mildly liberal. Center-shift rejection rates were therefore interpreted together with the corresponding source-internal and external-internal null rates. Detailed label-specific power curves and internal-null checks are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:app-dechao-center-results}.

To examine where the center-shift signal appeared within FC-128, we decomposed the embedding using the frozen classifier head. Let \(h(x)\in\mathbb{R}^{128}\) denote FC-128 and let
\[
\ell(x)=Wh(x)+b
\]
be the classifier logits. Because softmax is invariant to a common shift of all logits, we centered the classifier weights across classes:
\[
H_C=I_C-\frac{1}{C}\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^{\top},
\qquad
W_c=H_CW.
\]
We then defined
\[
h_{\mathrm{sem}}(x)
=
P_{\operatorname{row}(W_c)}h(x),
\qquad
h_{\mathrm{res}}(x)
=
h(x)-h_{\mathrm{sem}}(x).
\]
The first component affects class-relative logits, whereas
\[
W_ch_{\mathrm{res}}(x)=0,
\]
so the residual component is not used by the final linear classifier. This decomposition was used as a diagnostic of the detected shift, rather than as a new classifier.

At \(n=50\), reducing the label-8 residual representation to its leading eight principal components gave rejection rate 0.965, while an eight-dimensional random projection gave 0.8895; the full residual representation gave 0.975. A label-8-specific PCA sweep gave rejection rates 0.595, 0.925, and 0.960 for \(k=1,2,\) and \(4\) components, respectively. Thus most of the detectable residual shift was concentrated in a small number of directions. The dimension-matched and PCA-sweep diagnostics are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:app-dechao-residual-dimension}. We next examined whether the label-8 residual separation was associated with color or staining. A logistic classifier was first trained on residual features to distinguish source from external images, producing an out-of-sample source--external score. For each image, we also extracted 15 color summaries: the mean, standard deviation, and 10th, 50th, and 90th percentiles of each RGB channel. A separate regression was then fitted to predict the residual source--external score from these color summaries.

On held-out images, the color summaries explained \(72.8\%\) of the variation in the top-eight residual score and \(53.4\%\) of the variation in the full-residual score. Removing the color-predicted component reduced the corresponding source--external AUCs from \(0.778\) to \(0.622\) and from \(0.869\) to \(0.737\) (Figure~\ref{fig:app-dechao-color-score}). In a separate two-sample analysis using only the 15 color summaries, MMMD power reached \(0.960\) at \(n=50\).

Finally, source and external images were matched on a color-based propensity score before rerunning residual MMMD. After matching, rejection rates for both the top-eight and full residual representations fell to \(0.455\) at \(n=50\), while the maximum matched internal-null rejection rate was \(0.110\). Matching therefore removed a substantial fraction of the original residual separation, although the rejection rate remained above the internal-null reference (Figure~\ref{fig:app-dechao-color-mmmd}).

Taken together, the experiments show that CNN representations can improve MMMD power for the PathMNIST class-mixture alternative, but the most informative representation depends on the alternative. For label 8, the within-class source-vs-external difference was concentrated in low-dimensional residual directions and was strongly associated with color and staining summaries. The result is therefore described as a color/stain-associated residual domain shift, rather than evidence of a purely morphological difference. Additional diagnostics are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:dechao-cnn-mmmd}.


\section{Beyond task-trained CNNs: frozen visual encoders and stabilized testing}

This section studies whether the feature space used before testing changes the behavior of MMMD-style two-sample tests. Most comparisons keep the testing layer fixed, so differences in power mainly reflect differences in representation rather than changes in the statistic itself.

\subsection{From CNN features to foundation encoders}

The starting point is the CNN-MMMD pipeline: train a small CNN, extract feature vectors, and run MMMD on the resulting feature matrices. This works well when labels are available, but it also makes the test depend on a dataset-specific training step. A natural alternative is to use frozen visual encoders that already contain broad image structure.

We therefore compare raw pixels, task-trained CNN features, CLIP embeddings, and DINOv2 embeddings. CLIP provides an image-text contrastive representation, while DINOv2 provides a self-supervised visual representation that is often more sensitive to texture and morphology. In all cases, the two-sample test is applied after feature extraction; the encoders are not tuned on the test samples.

\subsection{Experimental protocol}

We report empirical power under mixture alternatives and empirical Type-I error under matched null settings. The experiments use three biomedical image benchmarks and four focused comparisons: BloodMNIST noise robustness, PathMNIST alignment with task-trained CNN baselines, NCT-CRC sample-size scaling, and a NCT-CRC testing-layer comparison between GEXP-5 and NEW-MMMD. Dataset descriptions, mixture definitions, and implementation paths are given in Appendix~\ref{app:repr}.

\subsection{BloodMNIST noise robustness}

The BloodMNIST experiment asks which representation survives additive image noise. Under the mixed task \((0,1,3)\) versus \((0,1,6)\), the task-trained CNN keeps power above \(0.91\) even at \(\sigma=1\), while CLIP and DINOv2 drop more sharply under heavy noise. Raw pixels are weaker at low and moderate noise, although they do not collapse as much at the largest noise level. Matched-null Type-I error stays close to nominal for the frozen encoders and is generally conservative for raw pixels and CNN features, so the power differences are not explained by uncontrolled false positives.

\subsection{PathMNIST alignment with task-trained CNNs}

The PathMNIST experiment asks whether frozen encoders can replace a task-trained CNN on small pathology patches. DINOv2 improves rapidly with sample size, rising from power \(0.0534\) at \(n=30\) to \(0.9904\) at \(n=150\), but small samples still favor the trained CNN baselines from the reproduction pipeline. CLIP improves more slowly, reaching power \(0.7172\) at \(n=150\). The matched-null runs are also conservative for DINOv2, with rejection rates about \(0.0038\) at \(n=60\) and \(0.0034\) at \(n=120\). For very small \(28\times 28\) pathology patches, a small CNN trained on the target distribution can still be better matched to the data than a frozen natural-image encoder.

\subsection{NCT-CRC sample-size scaling}

The NCT-CRC experiment shows how representation rankings can change with sample size. CNN features are competitive at small and medium \(n\), but DINOv2 becomes strongest once \(n\) is large enough. At \(n=150\), power is \(0.300\) for raw pixels, \(0.750\) for CLIP, \(0.875\) for CNN features, and \(0.988\) for DINOv2. This pattern suggests that DINOv2 features capture tissue architecture that raw pixels and CLIP do not capture as efficiently.

\subsection{Testing-layer stabilization with NEW-MMMD}

The final experiment separates the representation layer from the testing layer. With NCT-CRC representations fixed, GEXP-5 is compared with NEW-MMMD, which stabilizes covariance estimation and prunes redundant kernels. On DINOv2 features, NEW-MMMD is conservative at very small sample size but catches up by \(n=120\) and \(n=150\). Its clearest benefit is numerical: the raw covariance condition numbers for GEXP-5 are often on the order of \(10^6\) to \(10^7\), while the regularized NEW-MMMD condition numbers are much smaller after stabilization and pruning.

\subsection{Overall insights and limitations}

The main insight is that representation is part of the statistical design. Raw pixels leave the kernel test to work directly in a high-dimensional noisy space. Frozen encoders often help, especially DINOv2 on pathology-style images, but they are not uniformly best. A task-trained CNN can be strongest when labels are available and the target morphology is narrow enough for a simple supervised feature extractor to learn. NEW-MMMD improves calibration and matrix conditioning, but its power gains depend on sample size and representation.

These experiments are still limited by computational resources and time. The main goal is to examine whether foundation encoders, used as a representation layer, can substantially change the performance of two-sample testing. Therefore, the current study focuses on several representative feature spaces, including CLIP, DINOv2, raw pixels, and CNN features. Many directions remain open. Future work could compare more foundation models with different scales and training objectives, repeat the experiments on more medical image datasets, and test more class combinations, noise levels, and domain-shift settings. In addition, most encoders are currently frozen; fine-tuning or domain adaptation may further improve the power of MMMD-style tests.

\section{MMMD numerical extension: detailed figures and tables}
\label{app:numext}

All experiments in this section use synthetic data with dimension $d=30$, AR(1) covariance structure ($\rho=0.5$), and sample size $n=100$ unless noted otherwise. The testing layer uses the paper's multiplier bootstrap with $B=500$ draws per test and $N=150$--$200$ independent Monte Carlo trials per experimental condition. Three kernel families are examined: GEXP (Gaussian kernel with an exponential bandwidth grid), LAP (Laplace kernel with the same grid structure), and MIXED (concatenated Gaussian and Laplace kernels). The R implementation is organized as a modular library with a parallel backend for Monte Carlo replication.

\subsection{Contamination sensitivity}

Figure~\ref{fig:eps-sensitivity} shows how MMMD power varies with the heavy-tailed contamination fraction $\varepsilon$ under $P=\mathcal{N}(0,\Sigma)$ and $Q=(1-\varepsilon)\mathcal{N}(0,\Sigma)+\varepsilon\cdot t_{10}(0,\Sigma)$. When $\varepsilon=0$, the two populations are identical and rejection rates stay near the nominal level. As $\varepsilon$ increases from 0 to 1, the proportion of $t_{10}$ draws in $Q$ grows, and the distributional discrepancy becomes easier to detect. The power curve exhibits a clear sigmoidal shape, rising steeply between $\varepsilon=0.45$ (power 0.57) and $\varepsilon=0.65$ (power above 0.80). The minimum contamination detectable at power 0.80 is $\varepsilon_{\min}=0.625$.

From an applied perspective, this result means that MMMD can reliably flag a difference when roughly three-fifths or more of the second sample is drawn from a heavy-tailed distribution, even though both populations share the same mean and covariance structure. When contamination is below 40\%, the kernel-based statistic cannot reliably separate the null from the alternative at this sample size. This is consistent with the known difficulty of distinguishing Gaussian and $t_{10}$ distributions in moderate dimensions without large samples.

\subsection{Variance-scale sensitivity}

Figure~\ref{fig:var-sensitivity} and Table~\ref{tab:var-sensitivity} examine the minimum detectable covariance scaling. Under $P=\mathcal{N}(0,\Sigma_0)$ and $Q=\mathcal{N}(0,k\cdot\Sigma_0)$, the parameter $k>1$ controls the magnitude of the covariance inflation in the second population. All three MMMD kernel families (Mixed, Gauss, and LAP) detect $k_{\min}=1.25$ at power $\geq 0.80$, meaning a 25\% inflation in the overall covariance scale is sufficient for reliable detection. In contrast, the single-kernel Gaussian MMD requires $k_{\min}=1.40$, a 40\% inflation, to reach the same power threshold.

The gap is most striking at small effect sizes. At $k=1.20$ (a 20\% covariance increase), the multi-kernel methods achieve power 0.71--0.79, while the single-kernel baseline remains at 0.18. This roughly four-fold difference illustrates the practical value of multi-kernel aggregation: when the distributional shift is subtle relative to sampling variability, combining several bandwidths provides a more sensitive aggregate statistic than any single bandwidth can offer alone. The three MMMD variants perform similarly in this setting, suggesting that the aggregation mechanism (Mahalanobis weighting), rather than the specific kernel family, drives the gain over the single-kernel test.

\subsection{Type-I error calibration and ROC analysis}

Table~\ref{tab:type1} reports empirical Type-I error rates for the three kernel families at bandwidth counts $r\in\{3,5,10\}$. At moderate bandwidth counts ($r=3$ and $r=5$), all families are well-calibrated near the nominal $\alpha=0.05$. At $r=10$, a divergence appears: GEXP inflates to 0.120, exceeding twice the nominal rate; MIXED rises to 0.070; while LAP remains stable at 0.050. This pattern is consistent with the geometry of the kernel families. Gaussian kernels with close bandwidths produce near-identical Gram matrices, making the empirical covariance $\hat{\Sigma}$ nearly singular even after the paper's ridge regularization. Laplace kernels, with their heavier tails in the feature space, maintain better separation between neighbouring bandwidths, so the covariance estimate remains better conditioned at larger $r$.

Figure~\ref{fig:roc} provides the ROC perspective at a fixed variance-shift alternative ($k=1.25$). All three families trace similar ROC curves with false-positive rates (FPR) near 0.05 and true-positive rates (TPR) between 0.435 and 0.500. The fact that TPR values are comparable despite the Type-I differences at large $r$ is informative: the calibration advantage of LAP at $r=10$ does not translate into a substantial power advantage when the alternative is strong enough to be detected by any of the families. The practical guidance is to keep GEXP at $r\leq 5$ for well-calibrated inference, and to prefer LAP when a larger bandwidth bank is desired.

\subsection{Graph-MMMD: construction, calibration, and skewness robustness}

The Graph-MMMD extension replaces the Euclidean kernel with a diffusion-based kernel constructed from the sample graph. A $k$-nearest-neighbor graph is built on the pooled sample $\mathcal{X}_m\cup\mathcal{Y}_n$, the normalized graph Laplacian $L$ is formed, and the heat kernel $K_t=\exp(-tL)$ generates a family of kernels indexed by the diffusion time $t$. This construction is appealing for biomedical applications where the natural geometry of the data may be non-Euclidean --- for instance, when samples lie on a low-dimensional manifold or exhibit cluster structure that Euclidean distances do not capture well.

Figure~\ref{fig:graph-type1} validates the Type-I error of Graph-MMMD under four null distributions that differ in their tail weight and asymmetry: Gaussian AR(1) ($\rho=0.5$), scaled Gaussian ($k=1.5$), skew-normal with shape $\alpha=5$, and multivariate Laplace. Empirical rejection rates are 0.027, 0.020, 0.020, and 0.033, respectively --- all well below the nominal 0.05 level. The method is not inflated by either heavy tails (Laplace) or asymmetry (skew-normal).

Figure~\ref{fig:skew-type1} provides a finer-grained skewness sweep. The skew-normal shape parameter $\alpha$ is mapped to standardized skewness via the known moment formulas, and $\alpha$ is solved numerically by \texttt{uniroot()} to hit six target skewness levels from 0.00 (symmetric) to 0.90 (strongly skewed). Across the full range, Type-I error stays between 0.020 and 0.053 with no systematic upward or downward trend. This stability is noteworthy because many parametric tests that assume symmetry can become liberal under skewed nulls; Graph-MMMD shows no such sensitivity. The absence of monotonic drift also suggests that the graph Laplacian eigenstructure is not systematically altered by skewness in a way that biases the kernel-based statistic under $H_0$.

\subsection{Mixture distribution power reproduction}

Figure~\ref{fig:mixture} and Table~\ref{tab:mixture} reproduce the original paper's normal-$t$ mixture experiment (Fig.~2). The two populations are $P=(1-p)\mathcal{N}(0,\Sigma_0)+p\cdot t_{10}(0,\Sigma_0)$ and $Q=(1-p)\mathcal{N}(0,\Sigma_1)+p\cdot t_{10}(0,\Sigma_1)$ with $\Sigma_1=1.25\cdot\Sigma_0$, and the mixture proportion $p\in\{0,0.4,1.0\}$ controls how much of each sample is heavy-tailed. At $p=0$, the populations differ only through the 25\% covariance inflation, and Mixed MMMD achieves power 0.96 versus 0.46 for the single-kernel benchmark. At $p=0.4$, the gap narrows slightly to 0.80 versus 0.31. At $p=1.0$ (both populations are pure $t_{10}$), the power drops for all methods but the multi-kernel advantage persists: 0.58 versus 0.35.

Two patterns are worth noting. First, power decreases monotonically with $p$ for all methods, because the $t_{10}$ tails inject more variance into both samples and make the covariance difference harder to isolate. Second, the relative advantage of MMMD over the single-kernel test is roughly stable (2--5$\times$) across all three $p$ levels, confirming that the benefit of multi-kernel aggregation is not specific to a particular mixture proportion. This reproduction anchors our numerical extensions to the original paper's Figure~2 and confirms that our R implementation recovers the expected qualitative patterns.

Overall, these numerical extensions show that the Mahalanobis aggregation strategy generalizes well beyond the Gaussian mean-shift and variance-shift settings studied in the original paper. The multi-kernel advantage is most pronounced when the distributional difference is small relative to the noise, and the calibration properties are generally well-behaved provided the bandwidth count is kept moderate or the LAP family is used. The graph-based variant further demonstrates that the MMMD framework can accommodate non-Euclidean kernel constructions without compromising Type-I error control.
